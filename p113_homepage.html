What is P-113?

P-113 is a 12-amino-acid peptide corresponding to an active region of Histatin-5, a histidine-rich antimicrobial peptide naturally present in human saliva. Because of its short length, cationic character, and oral biological origin, P-113 has been widely discussed as a model peptide for studying peptide-mediated microbial inhibition in the oral environment.

P-113 is commonly represented as AKRHHGYKRKFH. Its amino acid composition includes basic residues that contribute to electrostatic interaction with microbial surfaces.

Antimicrobial Peptides: Scientific Background

Antimicrobial peptides are short, often cationic and amphipathic molecules that form part of innate host defense across many organisms. Their biological activities may involve microbial membrane interaction, intracellular targeting, immune modulation, and biofilm interference.

Cationic Charge

Many AMPs carry net positive charge, supporting attraction to negatively charged microbial membranes and cell-wall components.

Amphipathic Structure

Hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions can help AMPs associate with lipid membranes or microbial envelope structures.

Biofilm Relevance

Some AMPs can interfere with microbial adhesion, extracellular polymeric substance organization, or biofilm-associated cell viability.

AMP concept: cationic peptide + microbial surface interaction → membrane stress and/or biofilm modulation

Proposed Mechanistic Concepts

The antimicrobial behavior of P-113 and related Histatin-derived peptides is often interpreted through several complementary mechanisms. The exact dominant mechanism can vary depending on microbial species, buffer composition, pH, ionic strength, peptide concentration, and experimental model.

Membrane-Associated Activity

Like many AMPs, P-113 may interact with microbial cell envelopes through charge-based and hydrophobic interactions. This can contribute to altered membrane integrity or microbial stress responses.

Anti-Biofilm Activity

Biofilm studies often evaluate whether P-113 or modified analogs reduce microbial attachment, disrupt early-stage biofilm formation, or decrease biofilm-associated viability.
